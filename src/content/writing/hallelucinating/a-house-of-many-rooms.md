'This Album Changed My Life'

We're back, with a superbly written review by Uday Kanungo, for the album Michigan by Sufjan Stevens.

Why do I love Sufjan Stevens' Michigan? Let me count the ways.

Entering this album, is like finding yourself amidst an extended family assembled in a house gathering, a slew of siblings, every song a personality. There are sombre songs, parental in their sense of sacrifice and sadness ("For the Widows in Paradise") ; there are elaborate tunes, a group of mischievous cousins, experimental pranksters changing time signatures at whim ("All Good Naysayers"); there are silent hipsters and nerds, deep into crosswords and cubes ("Tahquamenon Falls"); there are bouncy teens, a spring in their step, a fizz of youth ("Upper Peninsula") and there are jilted lovers, aching like violin strings ("Romulus") there are optimistic uncles ("Say Yes! To Michigan), and jobless youth returned home, embarrassed in a gathering (Flint, For the Unemployed and Underpaid). Before he hit his crescendo with Chicago, here is Sufjan in all his brilliant breadth. Sure, it's a loose, baggy album, anything goes. But the range, musical and emotional, in it's mellowness, never fails me. The way he composes all these little dollops, one always feels rocking and swaying in the folds of time, and he never does it too much. You feel in safe and secure hands, you will never fall into chaos. Its like he sees all the possible routes the song can take in slo-mo, plucks it out of thin air, and carefully combines and selects it to the great joy of the one who listens. You can sometimes predict some little thing before it actually appears, a little turn of melody, a sweet lyric and there's no beauty like seeing that prophecy fulfilled.

The portrait of this artist is incomplete without this album. He's a great host to every guest with an earphone and a ear. When you enter Sufjan's house, you get his best wine, the best seat in the house, the best attention.

Stevens is an effing genius. Repeat till the page is full, printer.
