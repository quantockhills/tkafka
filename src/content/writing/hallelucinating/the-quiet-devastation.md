Fiction review - ‘Never Let Me Go’ by Kazuo Ishiguro.

I remember watching the TV with my grandmother as a kid. In retrospect, quite a bit of what we saw used to be shallow and only mildly entertaining, but I stuck around because I got to spend time with her. Anyways, a vivid memory of those times (I would've been six years old) is of us watching this show where the protagonist dies. And I hadn't got the intricacies of death nailed down just yet, so I prodded the truth out of my parents. It left me scared to imagine that one day, I too would die - it was a simple feeling of dread, nothing more, nothing less.

The reason I mention this is, I found myself recounting that memory as I got done with the book... It has that air of death and gangrene to it, and can often come across as gloomy and desolate.

I loved the way the first two parts of the book were written; the idyllic atmosphere of Hailsham are so well described. There is a certain warmth and happiness to these pages, although certain incidents do point to how Ishiguro is going to take your feelings, step all over them and make it hurt just enough for you to recover, and repeat this until the end whence a sharp and short blow suffices.

At times horrifying, at times warm (but mostly the former). I'd defer from making grandiose statements such as 'the purpose of books is to...', but Never Let Me Go made me feel things I didn't know I was capable of feeling, which is what I expect from most of the art that I enjoy. There's gloom, definitely - but in the build up to that is human warmth, friendship and coping with depressing situations in a positive way. The underlying romance was a touching element too, and forms the backbone of the third part of the story.

Thus there exists a beautiful contrast between two halves of the book; a 'before' and 'after', almost. You climb a certain cliff and fall down way below where you initially were. I cannot say a lot more without giving the plot away, but personally I loved the book. Read this if you enjoy having your feelings messed with (or if you generally enjoy well written books and don't mind them getting dark).
