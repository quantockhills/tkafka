On my first listen, I didn’t see Funeral as anything special or even cathartic. At that time it felt like a collection of standard indie rock, 4/4 beats and simple guitar strums.

I kept revisiting it on and off throughout college (at the suggestion of certain people) and grew to love little moments initially. Going for a walk at 6 in the morning to ‘Tunnels’ was a wonderful experience – the violins and guitars keep the momentum and you don’t feel like stopping. Speaking of ‘Tunnels’, what a track! It is suited for those moments of change – early morning revelations about yourself and your path ahead, for welcoming the winter, for your first love. And vocalist Win Butler has a poetic flair to the way he writes. ‘You change all the lead sleeping in my head to gold’, he sings. It is the sound of youth, the good with the bad – the wonder, the romance, the anxiety and the uncertainty of youth condensed into songs that you can shout at the top of your voice to. Being about youth, there are also parts when the singer realizes that growing up is inevitable; as we grow older we tend to face loss in ways we were shielded from before - ‘My family tree’s losing all its leaves’, Regine sings on ‘Learning to Drive’. Some songs act as a call to arms, bringing to notice all that has been lost in the process of growing up – optimism, aggression, for some even vitality; ‘Now that I’m older, my heart’s colder and I can see that it’s a lie’. I had this theory of firsts. I think that there’s a lot of joys in (the later stages of?) youth that you get to experience for the first time. Such as your first kiss, first Murakami novel, first Radiohead album. First long run, first time performing at a concert, first house party. These firsts are what keep life fresh and new, keep the wonder alive. But I fear that as I grow old, these firsts will slow down in how frequently they appear; after all, there are only so many new experiences that life has to offer.

I feel the same fear reflected in the album. As for the cure? 'Wake up, hold your mistake up, before they turn the summer into dust… I guess we'll just have to adjust' (from 'Wake Up'). Not exactly an optimistic approach, but an honest one. I wonder if this honesty is just a part of growing up.

Don't get me wrong, there is joy to be found too. Joy in the beautiful string section of 'Rebellion (Lies)', or in the moment when you think 'Une Anne Sans Lumiere' has ended, but it picks up pace in what is perhaps my favorite moment of the album. There's celebration and triumph, and I guess they are celebrating the fact that we are still here, still alive. Celebration of the new found honesty, and the fact that our ability to love is still intact (and that the moments of love are what make life joyous). Celebration of impermanence, of learning to accept loss. Of the knowledge that while life ahead will definitely be different, we are capable enough to handle the challenges. In this vitality lies the wonder of Funeral. And I think that is why I keep coming back to it.
