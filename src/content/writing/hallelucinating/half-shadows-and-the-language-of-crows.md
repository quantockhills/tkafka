Fiction review - ‘Kafka on The Shore’ by Haruki Murakami. Murakami books are movements in themselves. Kafka is a catalyst for change, very much like music by the Archduke trio the book mentions, something which when experienced can make you a different person. There's so many recurring themes and self references, such vivid symbolism - the crows, the butterfly (one of the best lines in the book likens writing the song 'Kafka on the Shore' to holding a butterfly by the wings without disturbing its motion), the distant room where you can find chords, the ancient Greek legend about us being only half of a whole and likening this to the main characters only having half a shadow, the repetitive reminders to conquer your fear and anger, and of everyone needing a place they can come back to. The images are repeated at various points throughout, pounding their way into your head eventually.

The book is chaotic, lacks order and can get somewhat scatterbrained, but this works out in its favor in a way. A uniting theme, if you're looking for one, is how some people need to go to the farthest edges of sanity to better fit in the world around them. Like any decent Murakami though, it can be tough to get through, and is not a book to be read in one sitting. It is, however, a book to pick up when you have time, and unwrap slowly. The more you think of it, the more you can unravel... there is always something new to be found, something that kept me coming back. Very much like Kafka, I'm not sure if I consciously learnt anything from the ordeal, but the journey, in a way, changed my perspective on the self. Again, words don't really do it justice. As Nakata says, you have to get there to understand it.
