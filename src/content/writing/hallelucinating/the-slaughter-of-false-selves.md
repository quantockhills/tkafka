‘I tend to break down when I make a record. You have to destroy the versions of yourself that provide too easy a solution. Someone comes along in yourself, and he has a slogan, he has a view on love, a position on the world... To murder all those false persons that arise and try to tell you what the song is, to get to that place where you can defend every word... that takes a slaughter. I write to murder the selves that whisper untruths to me.’ – Leonard Cohen

Lately, I find myself drawn to Cohen’s art, not only in terms of his music – which has been endlessly dissected – but his writing process which holds much inspiration for those seeking.

At their most tender, Cohen’s musings were spiritual, solemn – such reverence and warmth were not reserved for a higher form of consciousness, but extended to his lovers, and equally to the lovelorn and the broken. He wrote of years spent alone with oneself, a graceful balance of meditation and yearning. It would be reductive to call his music melancholic because he celebrated so much of the beauty that hides behind the veil of melancholy, he conveyed ways to act with grace in these moments of suffering.

At their darkest, the songs questioned – with sharpness and precision – the very form of consciousness that he prayed to, immortalized. This was a cynical, whiskey-soaked side to him, often conveyed with a gruff baritone. You could almost picture him in a suit, smoking a cigar at some local bar, contemplating his place in the world.

‘If you are the dealer, let me out of the game. If you are the healer, it means I am broken and lame.’ he sings in ‘You Want it Darker’, a song from his final album of the same name, just weeks before his death.

The music arose from his relentless devotion to his craft. ‘A song will yield if you stick with it long enough’, he once said, before going on to warn, ‘but long enough is way beyond any reasonable duration’. A sense of seriousness, of weighing each word with care, permeates through his work.

Paradoxically enough, along with the solemnity that exists on the surface, there is also a sense of not taking oneself too seriously, a tendency to laugh at the very questions – the heavy, existential questions – that Cohen spent most of his life investigating. The poem ‘Thousands’ (included in the photo album) reflects this wry humor.
