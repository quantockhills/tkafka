This here is a review of 'Hand. Cannot. Erase.' by Steven Wilson, written by Shivangi. I met 'Hand. Cannot. Erase.' in January 2017 in a hostel in another city. I say met because I only heard a familiar voice (ref: Lazarus) but very differently compelling lyrics in the background while having breakfast with fifteen strangers before I complimented the person for his playlist who was later kind enough to let me go through it, which is when I was introduced to the album. I say met also because listening to this album is like meeting a part of so many people you already know, including yourself.

Wilson wrote 'Hand. Cannot. Erase.' taking inspiration from the case of Joyce Carol Vincent, a woman who died in her north London apartment and wasn't missed by anyone for three years. contrary to what we may think, Carol was an outgoing and popular woman. Wilson captures the isolation of our modern lives in this album which is nothing short of an epic. it explores ideas of alienation in cities, nostalgia of childhood, loneliness, absurdity of the 21st century, internet and so on. some tracks will hit you like a story by Kafka would. or if you're a Radiohead fan, you'll get hints of 'OK Computer' too. each song, as is the case for any progressive rock track, is on an average 10-12 minutes long which is perfect because these songs are literally stories and they need time to be recited and you need time to take them in.

Wilson's protagonist is a woman and he has written the tracks from her perspective; they range from internal monologues to letters to diary entries, anything that is an explosion of her thoughts which is probably what inspired the album art too. Katherine Jenkins from Wales and Ninet Tayeb from Israel have contributed in two tracks where the voice of the woman was literally required. instead of reading more while I harp about the beauty of this album, you can just go and listen to it and enjoy (includes crying) the rollercoaster that is 'Hand. Cannot. Erase.'

and while you're at it, also listen to 'The Raven That Refused to Sing (And Other Stories)'
